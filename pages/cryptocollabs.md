# Building a Guidebook for Collaborations between Cryptographers and Social Good Organizations

There has been growing interest in cryptography for social good, as demonstrated by seminars like [Community Driven Crypto](https://communitydrivencrypto.github.io/) and workshops such as [Re-Imagining Cryptography and Privacy (ReCAP)](https://recapworkshop.online/index.html). 
These initiatives prioritize building secure tools for society and often explore how cryptographic systems can protect the privacy of marginalized communities. 
They also encourage cryptographers to collaborate with organizations that may be technologically resource-constrained.

The values of this movement are reflected in several collaborations between cryptographers and such organizations. For example, the [Boston Women’s Workforce Council](https://thebwwc.org/mpc) partnered with cryptographers from Boston University to build a secure multi-party computation system for analyzing gender and racial pay disparities. This work was later extended to study workplace culture in museums in collaboration with Museums Moving Forward (MMF) [Kaptchuk et al](https://youtu.be/l-HrnrnB6dU?si=tkpvlqMqb2q_y5gs). Callisto, an organization focused on combating sexual misconduct, worked with cryptographers to develop a privacy-preserving system for identifying serial perpetrators [Rajan et al](https://dl.acm.org/doi/abs/10.1145/3209811.3212699). Cryptographers from EPFL and CISPA also collaborated with the International Committee of the Red Cross to design a secure method for distributing humanitarian aid in Geneva, Switzerland [Wang et al](http://arxiv.org/abs/2303.17343). 

Although these collaborations target different societal issues, they share a common structure: partnerships between technically expert cryptographers and organizations with limited technical resources. As such, both parties have likely encountered communication and coordination challenges. [Kaptchuk et al.](https://youtu.be/l-HrnrnB6dU?si=tkpvlqMqb2q_y5gs) begins to explore these dynamics, recounting lessons from their collaboration with MMF in a presentation that offers insights for others pursuing similar work.

While Kaptchuk et al.’s contribution marks an important first step in documenting and sharing experiences from this kind of interdisciplinary engagement, it represents only one perspective. Many other collaborative efforts are likely to offer equally valuable lessons. Yet, there remains a lack of consolidated guidance or best practices for cryptographers who wish to engage with resource-constrained organizations.

Moreover, Kaptchuk et al.’s work primarily highlights the cryptographers’ viewpoint. To fully understand these collaborations, it's also essential to learn from the organizations involved. Their perspectives can shed light on institutional challenges, inform better practices, and empower other organizations seeking similar partnerships.

As Rogaway has argued, cryptographers should consider how their work can serve the public good rather than solely pursue capitalist or self-referential goals. If we aim to foster more effective and equitable collaborations, it is vital to build on the lessons of past efforts. Otherwise, new partnerships risk repeating avoidable challenges already encountered by those who came before.
